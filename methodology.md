# How this archive is built

This archive is updated every night. A search runs across eight open-access databases, looking for new strategy documents and evaluation reports related to social and behaviour change programmes in global health. When a new document is found, the full text is extracted. Rhetorical commitments are identified — direct quotes where an organisation pledges to work in a specific way. Each quote is then reviewed a second time to check whether it represents a genuine operational commitment or just aspirational language. A plain-English summary is written. Then the document enters the archive.

The search covers eight databases. OpenAlex indexes open-access scholarly literature globally. CORE aggregates research outputs from repositories worldwide. PubMed and PMC hold biomedical literature from the US National Library of Medicine. Europe PMC covers life sciences literature, including grey literature from funders and institutions. Semantic Scholar is an academic search engine covering over 200 million papers. The World Bank Open Knowledge Repository holds development reports and evaluations. ReliefWeb collects humanitarian reports from NGOs and UN agencies. WHO IRIS is the World Health Organization's institutional repository.

The five promise categories were not invented for this project. They come from three decades of SBC programme review literature — from researchers who spent careers documenting what global health communication programmes said versus what they delivered. The categories are: community ownership, context sensitivity, participation, sustainability, and equity. Every strategy document in the archive makes at least one of these promises. Most make all five.

The review step exists to filter out noise. A second pass challenges each extracted quote, asking: is this a real operational commitment, or just an aspiration phrased as one? Each quote is scored from 1 to 5. Quotes scoring below 3 are removed. This step reduces false positives significantly. It does not eliminate them.

The credibility score shown on each document is the average judgment across the quotes that survived review. It is not a peer review. It does not verify the document against external sources. It does not check whether the promise was kept. It only measures how concrete and specific the language of commitment is.

Strategy documents receive editorial illustrations because the promise itself is the subject — abstract, aspirational, worth examining on its own. Evaluation documents are referenced in the aggregate charts because their findings belong in context alongside other data, not isolated as standalone images.

The "pending editorial review" badge means the document was added to the archive and has not yet been checked by a researcher. Every new document carries this badge until someone reads the source, confirms the quotes are accurate, and marks it as reviewed. Pairing a strategy document with its corresponding evaluation is also a manual step. The archive finds documents. Matching promises to outcomes is research work that requires a human.

This archive has real limits. It only contains open-access documents. Anything behind a paywall is invisible. The system cannot verify that an extracted quote appears at the exact position claimed in the original. Context around a quote can change its meaning in ways that are not always caught. The archive has not been validated against a gold-standard human-coded dataset. Grey literature from national governments and smaller NGOs is underrepresented because these organisations often do not deposit their reports in searchable repositories.

All quotes in this archive are exact extracts from source documents, not paraphrases or summaries. If a quote looks wrong, the source link is there. Check it.
