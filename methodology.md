# How this works

Eight software agents run every night at 3 AM UTC. They search academic databases for new documents about social and behaviour change in global health. They extract the full text. They identify rhetorical promises - direct quotes where organisations commit to specific ways of working. A second agent challenges every quote, asking whether it is a genuine operational commitment or just aspiration dressed up as one. A third writes a plain-English summary. A fourth decides whether the document gets an editorial illustration or feeds into the aggregate charts. Then the data file updates, the charts regenerate, and the site rebuilds.

The agents search eight databases. OpenAlex indexes open-access scholarly literature globally. CORE aggregates open-access research outputs from repositories worldwide. PubMed and PMC hold biomedical literature from the US National Library of Medicine. Europe PMC covers life sciences literature including grey literature from funders and institutions. Semantic Scholar uses AI to index and connect academic papers. The World Bank Open Knowledge Repository holds development reports and evaluations. ReliefWeb collects humanitarian reports and evaluations from NGOs and UN agencies. WHO IRIS is the World Health Organization's institutional repository.

The five promise categories were not invented for this project. They come from three decades of SBC programme review literature. Scholars who spent careers documenting what global health communication programmes said versus what they delivered identified these recurring themes: community ownership, context sensitivity, participation, sustainability, and equity. Every strategy document in the archive makes at least one of these promises. Most make all five.

The adversarial review step exists because language models are eager to please. A first model extracts quotes. A second model challenges them. It asks: is this a real operational commitment, or just an aspiration phrased as one? Each quote gets scored 1 to 5. Quotes scoring below 3 are removed. This step reduces false positives significantly. It does not eliminate them.

The credibility score shown on each document is the average adversarial judgment across the quotes that survived review. It is not a peer review. It does not verify the document against external sources. It does not check whether the promise was kept. It only measures how concrete and specific the language of commitment is.

Strategy documents get editorial illustrations because the promise itself is the subject. The illustration is abstract, geometric, aspirational - it represents the rhetoric, not the reality. Evaluation documents are referenced in the aggregate charts because their findings belong in context alongside other data, not isolated as standalone images.

The amber "pending editorial review" badge means the document was added by the pipeline and has not been checked by a human. Every automatically added document carries this badge until someone reads the original, confirms the quotes are accurate, and sets reviewed to true in the data file. Pairing a strategy document with its evaluation using the paired_evaluation_id field is also a manual step. The agents find documents. They do not yet match promises to outcomes.

This archive has real limits. It only contains open-access documents. Anything behind a paywall is invisible. The system cannot verify that an extracted quote appears at the exact position claimed in the original. Context around a quote can change its meaning in ways the model does not catch. The pipeline has not been validated against a gold-standard human-coded dataset. Grey literature from national governments and small NGOs is underrepresented because these organisations often do not deposit their reports in searchable repositories.

All quotes in this archive are exact extracts from source documents, not paraphrases or summaries. If a quote looks wrong, the source link is there. Check it.
